## CHAPTER 3 : WHO AM I?

#### WHAT IS CONSCIOUSNESS?
- The question "Who am I" is a fundamentally philosophical question. It is a question about consciousness.
- We can't detect consciousness or lack of consciousness directly. 
	- Bc its hard to know as a third person if someone or something is acting out of consciously or unconsciously.
- Consciousness is the ability to have subjective experiences inside a mind - and not merely appearing to do so.
	- In philosophy they call this **qualia**. A person's qualia cannot be detected from the outside.
- Our ethical judgements often hinge on our assessments of consciouness. (e.g. animal rights)
	- Ethical questions on what beings have rights hinge on whether those entities have subjective experiences.
- Debate on consciousness is binary but Kurzweil wants to talk about the degree
	-  In 2012 the Cambridge Declaration on Consciousness was publicly proclaimed. According to the declaration, all mammals, birds, and other creatures have neurological substrates that generate consciousness.
- Complex brain give rise to functional consciousness but what causes us to have subjective consciousness?
- As tech gives us ability to expand our consciousness, we'll need to decide what we believe generates the qualia at the core of our identity and focus on preserving it.
	- Brain that can support more 'sophisticated behavior' give rise to more sophisticated subjective consciousness.
		- Sophisticated behavior arises from the complexity of information processing in a brain and this in turn is largely determined by how flexibly it can represent information and how many hierarchical layers are in its network.
- The tech advancement has enabled us to access majority of discoveries made by humans but we are experiencing them at a level of consciousness similar to our Neolithic anscestors. When we can augment the neocortex in 2030s or 2040s, we will be deepening our subjective consciousness.

#### ZOMBIES, QUALIA, AND THE HARD PROBLEM OF CONSCIOUSNESS
- There's no means for us to compare our qualia using language or any kind of communication.
	- = Science cannot tell the difference between zombies and normal humans.
		- e.g. There's no way of telling how I perceive the color green is the same as another person.
- Most ppl say hurting a dog(w/ subjective consciouseness) is worse than hurting a zombie(w/o subjective consciouseness)
	- If zombies are theoretically possible, then there must be no necessary causal connection between qualia and the physical systems that do the information processing that outwardly give the appearance of consciousness.

- Subjective consciousness is qualitatively different from the realm of physical laws and it doesn't follow that particular pattern of information processing according to these laws would yield conscious experience at all.
	- This is what David Chalmers calls the hard problem. To get answers he turns to a philosophical idea he calls the "panprotopsychism"
- Panprotopsychism(범원형심론) treats consciousness like a fundamental force of the universe. 
	- Although we'll never be able to prove this scientifically, there's a powerful ethical imperative to act as though it is true. If one assumes that something is conscious if there's a chance that it could be conscious.
- From a panprotopsychism view, Turing test would not just serve to establish human level functional capability but would also furnish strong evidence for subjective consciousness and moral rights.
	- The political system will adapt fast enough to enshrine such rights in law by the time the first Turing-level AI are developed. Thus, it will be responsible for the people to develop them to formulate ethical frameworks that can restrain abuses.
- Panprotopsychism strikes the middle ground between dualism and physicalism(=materialism).
	- Dualism argues that consciousness arises from totally separate kind of stuff than ordinary dead matter which dualists identify as a "soul"
	- Physicalism suggests that consciousness arise wholly from certain arrangements of ordinary physical matter in our brains.

#### DETERMINISM, EMERGENCE, AND THE FREE WILL DILEMMA
- A concept closely related to consciousness is our sense of free will. People usually define free will as the ability to control their own actions.
	- However, philosophers believe that existence of free will requires that the future not be predetermined. 
- "Chance is as relentless as necessity" Simon Blackburn
	- Blackburn argues that if an action of mine is the result of a "random fluke" in my brain rather than a determined cause, I am still not the author of that action.
		- Necessity: "You did this because you had to." vs. Chance: "This happened to you by accident."
		- In both scenarios, "I" (the conscious agent) am not in control. If my arm moves because of a random neural firing (chance), it is just as "relentless" and indifferent to my desires as if it moved because of a mechanical reflex (necessity).
- A meaningful concept of free will must somehow synthesize both deterministic and indeterministic philosophical ideas. (=cellular automata)
- Stephen Wolfram sheds light on phenomena that have both deterministic and indeterministic properties called "cellular automata"
- Cellular automata are simple models represented by cells that alternate between states(e.g. black/white, dead/alive) based on one of many sets of rules.
	- For each cell there are 8 possible combinations of states for the three cells bordering it in the previous step. The rule specifies which state each of these combinations causes in the step that comes after it.
- Most of the world can be explained by four differenct classes, distinguished by the kinds of results they produce(Class 1 to Class 4). The most interesting is the class 4.
	- The point is that there is no way to determine what the thousandth or milionth row will be other than to compute them one by one.
	- Systems based on class 4 properties possess an irreducible complexity that defies the old reductive versions of determinism.
	- It demonstrates a property called "emergence". It means simples things collectively giving rise to much more complex things. 
- We live in a world that is deeply affected by the kind of patterning found in such cellular automata
	- A very simple algorithm producing highly complex behavior between order and chaos.
	- It is this complexity in us that may give rise to consciousness and free will.
- Wolfram thinks laws of physics themselves arise from some kinds of computational rules related to cellular automata.
	- In 2020, he announced Wolfram Physics Project in effort to understand all of physics via a generalized cellular automata model
- If reality is based on class 4 rules, there would be no summarizing algorithm that could predict the exact state of the entire universe at some time in the future.
	- In other words, there is no way to unfold reality without letting it actually go forward.
	- It also means being able to replicate the function of the brain does not imply an ability to pre-compute its future states.
	- If consciousness can emerge only from the kind of order-and-chaos complexity of class 4 automata, this can be seen as a philosophical argument for why we exist.
- This opens door to the concept compatibilism. "Compatibilism" is a view that a deterministic world can still be a world with free will.
	- We arise from deeper forces, but our choices cannot be known in advance, so we have free will as long as the processes that give rise to our consciousness are able to be expressed through our actions in the world.

#### THE FREE WILL DILEMMA OF MORE THAN ONE BRAIN PER HUMAN
- Human brain consists of two hemispheres, left and right. 
	- Even if a person only has one side of the brain, he/she can function normally.
	- If one has two brains but w/o corpus callosum(a thick bundle of nerve fibers that connects the left and right hemispheres of brain), meaning both brains operate but have no means to communicate between them, it would confabulate plausible-sounding explanations for why it claimed to have made a decision.
- There are many types of decision makers within us that could have a free will.
	- e.g. Neocortex consists of many smaller modules.
	- Minsky described our brains as a "society of mind".
- Although there are scientific advances, we still do not understand how neural processes work to make conscious decisions.

#### "YOU 2" IS CONSCIOUS. IS IT YOU?
- If someone made a complete computerized replica of ur brain and it claims to be conscious, there is no plausible scientific basis for denying its concsiousness.
- If the case above were to happen, here are some Qs we must answer...
	- First of all, is this replica(You 2) *really* you?
		- Since the replica would be able to act independently, diverging from the original version of You, You 2 would not be You.
	- If someone were to gradually replace each section of the brain with a digital copy, is the new You after such replacement still you? How about when at the end all of your brain is entirely replaced with a digital copy?
		- Ship of Theseus : An experiment in Ancient Greek philosophy that asks a fundamental question. If one replaces every single part of an object, is it still the same object? 
	- In the scenario where we gradually shift the information in your brain onto a non-biological substrate, we have strong reason to believe that your subjective consciousness will be preserved.
	- If the digital brain doesn't diverge from the biological one, then there was never a moment when they existed as separate entities. The difference in continuity is how we can reconcile that you remains as you.
	- In the third scenario, our brains are almost completely replaced within a few months. So in fact, you are a biological version of you two compared with yourself a few months ago.
- What keeps your identity intact is information and function, not any particular structure or material.
- Just like a river, the water molecules passing through a given slice of the river are completely different every few milliseconds, but we still define it as the same river.
- The continuity of our information pattern will make each of us feel like the person we are today.
- Since there will be no way to objectively determine the truth, our legal and ethical systems would likely have to treat both as separate entities.

#### THE INCREDIBLE UNLIKELINESS OF BEING
- It is quite awe-inspiring if we think of the extraordinary chain of unlikely events that enabled each one of us to come into being.
	- Your parents had to meet. But before that both sets of your grandparents would have to meet. Before that, all four sets of your great-grandparents, and eight sets of your great-great-grandparents, and so on. All these things would have to happen just to make *you*.
- The universe coming into being with the ability to evolve complex information is even more unlikely.
	- According to the standard model of particle physics, there are 37 kinds of elementary particles. These particles interact according to four fundamental forces: gravity, electromagnetism, the strong nuclear force, and the weak nuclear force. There is also the hypothetical graviton, which some scientists believe is responsible for gravitational effects.
		- These interactions define the rules of physics.

- The prevailing view among biologists is that life arose on Earth through abiogenesis.
	- According to this theory, non-living matter, over a long time, naturally combined into complex building blocks of life-enabling proteins. Eventually, proteins came together in a pattern that enabled them to self-replicate, which is the *origin of life*.
- Observer selection bias = Anthropic principle
	- The idea that the universe must have the physical constants and laws that allow for life, simply because we are here to observe it.

#### AFTER LIFE
- GAN(Generative Adversarial Networks) entails two neural networks competing against each other.
	- The first tries to generate an example from a target class. The second tries to discriminate what the first has created and the other. The first is rewarded for fooling the second, and the second is rewarded for making accurate judgments.
	- This process repeats many times without human supervision, and both neural nets gradually increase their proficiency.
- Through these techniques, AI can imitate specific person's writing style, replicate their voice, or realistically replace a face in a video.
	- Not only will these capabilities improve greatly in the coming years, but their conversions will create simulations that are more realistic than the sum of its parts.
- Replicating appearance, behavior, skills of a person who has passed away is a phenomenon that Kurzweil calls "afterlife technology."
	- In the late 2020s, advanced AI will be able to create a very lifelike replicant. They would only recreate the function, not the form of the information that had been in the person's brain.
	- Realistic bodies and actual reality will be also possible using nanotechnology of the late 2030s.
	- But once nanotechnology allows molecular-scale engineering, we'll be able to create vastly more advanced artificial bodies than what biology allows.
- The introduction of replicants will pose many challenging social and legal questions, such as:
	* Are they to be considered people with full human and civil rights?
	* Are they responsible for contracts signed or crimes previously committed by the person they are replicating?
	* Under what conditions should the creation of replicants be restricted or banned?
- The replicants would have cognition as complex as a natural biological person. They will be conscious, and they will think that they are that person.
	- If you believe identity is fundamentally about information like memories and personalities, the replicants would indeed be the same person.
- In the early 2040s, nanobots will be able to go into a living person's brain and make copy of all the data that forms the memories and personality of the original person = You 2
- Each of us will have to make decisions about using this technology based on our own philosophical or spiritual values.
- Simulating a mind on a non-biological medium can mean vastly different things in computational terms. Brain emulations fall into roughly five categories:
	1. Functional emulations: would act like a biologically based mind but need not replicate the specific computational structure of a given person’s brain.
	2. Connectomic emulations: replicate the hierarchical connections and logical relationships between groups of neurons but need not model every single cell.
	3. Cellular emulations: simulate key information about every neuron in a brain but do not simulate detailed physical forces inside.
	4. Biomolecular emulations: model interactions between proteins and tiny dynamic forces within each cell.
	5. Quantum emulations: capture subatomic effects within and between molecules.
- The practical goal is to figure out how to get computers to interface effectively with the brain and crack the code of how the brain represents information.

#### WHO CAN I BE
- Identity are tightly interconnected with issues of consciousness, free will, and determinism.
- In the future, our behaviors can align with our values, our lives will not be cut short by the failings of biology. Finally, humans can be truly responsible for who we are.
