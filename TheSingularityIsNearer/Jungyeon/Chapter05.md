## CHAPTER 5 : THE FUTURE OF JOBS

#### THE CURRENT REVOLUTION
- It was irrelevant for Luddites that higher paying jobs had been created.
	- Also there were no govt programs to retrain them
	- Luddites became a symbol of those who protest being left behind by technological progress
- However, positive result of this early wave of automation was that common person could now afford well made goods. Also the fact that whole new industry were formed.

#### DESTRUCTION AND CREATION
- There's political anxiety associated with automation bc we don't know what industries or jobs are being created. It doesn't matter to an individual that there will be more jobs in the future.
- Looking at the past, dramatic reduction in many categories of employment the total number of jobs has grown dramatically
- Ppl are now working fewer hrs and making more money
	- Although median incomes are much lower. Some of this difference reflects extremely high earnings by a minority of the population
- The second wave of Industial Revolution brought a new mass of workers into manufacturing around the start of the 20th century
	- US workforce maintained about 1 in 4 ppl in manufacturing until the 1970s. For five decades it remained somewhat similar.
	- The two technology related shifts that began to erode the US factory employment:
	1) Innovations in logistics and transportation : containerized shipping made it cheaper for companies to outsource manufacturing to countries with less expensive labor
	2) automation reduced the amount of human labor that is needed : as computerization transformed production, the hourly output of the avg manufacturing worker doubled
- Despite the dramatic reduction in both farm and factory jobs the US labor force has been steadily growing
- As the economy has shifted toward more tech intensive jobs we have been increasing investment in education.
	- We spend more than 18x as much today in constant dollars per child in K-12 as we did a century ago
	- The technological change has replaced most jobs in the economy serveral times over(aided by improving education) We have seen sustained and dramatic economic progress, despite the perception that major categories of employment are about to disappear.

#### IS THIS TIME DIFFERENT?
- Unlike previous technology-driven transitions the latest automation will result in a loss of more jobs than it creates
	- Economist sees this as a culmination of several successive waves of change
	1) First wave: deskilling
	- It becomes easier for people to take new jobs w/o lengthy training
		- e.g. drivers of carriage who needed extensive skills was replaced by automobile drivers
	- low paying jobs replaced higher paying jobs (e.g. craftsmen -> factory worker)
	2) Second wave: upskilling
	- often follows deskilling and introduces tech that require more skill than what came before
		- e.g. manufacturing machines that take a larger role in manufacturing but require sophisticated skills
 	- this wave tends to replace low paying jobs with high paying jobs
	3) Oncoming wave: nonskilling
	- This wave takes humans out of the equation
	- This is what makes AI driven innovation different from previous techs
- Its important to distinguish between tasks and professions 
	- In some cases tasks that are wholly automated allow a certain type of job to pivot to a different set of tasks (upskilling)
	- As tech advances human graphic designers will spend less time physically sketching art and more time brainstorming ideas
- In places where the wages are relatively low, it makes more sense to use labor intensive process. Where wages are high there's more incentive to innovate and design machines that require less labor.
	- This is why Great Britain was the cradle of the Industrial Revolution
- While machines can be one time purchases that become assets, employee wages are ongoing costs, so businesses have incentives to automate their operations.
- Productivity Puzzle
	- If tech change cause net job losses, one would predict that there would be fewer hours worked for a given level of economic output. Then productivity would be markedly increasing.
	- Yet starting from 2004 productivity growth began to significantly slow.
	- This has been one of the greatest economic mysteries of the past decade
	- It may be bc we don't count the exponentially increasing value of information products in GDP
- Many IT have become vastly more useful while prices have stayed more or less constant
- Nominal wealth and income increases over the past few decades do not properly reflect the massive lifestyle advantages enabled by new tech.
	- GDP naturally counts today's $900 chip as equivalent to one produced over two decades ago
	- This dynamic is stronger for digital goods and services (e.g. Google's services)
		- Digital service usually give the public free access and cover their marginal costs with ads
- Even though the services are free to consumers we can approximate people's willingness to pay for them by looking at their choices (AKA consumer surplus)
	- e.g. earn $20 mowing lawn vs. spend time on tiktok. If one chooses tiktok then tiktok is giving you at least $20 of value
- People place enormous value on social media usage but only a small fraction of that value is visible to economists as revenue
	- As digital tech takes up a larger share of the economy, consumer surplus is rising much faster than GDP suggests
	- Less pollution, safer living conditions, expanded opportunities for learning and entertainment are also some areas that are enabled by technological change but doesn't show up on GDP
- Advanced AI will bring down costs for products that are not considered IT like health-care cost, cost of food, etc.
	- In 2030s it will be relatively inexpensive to live at a level that is considered luxurious today
	- Tech driven deflation in all these areas will only widen the gap between nominal productivity and the real avg benefit that each hr of human work brings to society

- Why does economic data show that the proportion of Americans in the workforce is shrinking?
	- The decline is likely due to automation
	- More people are educated and staying in school longer
	- Baby boomer generation goes into retirement
	- If we take a look at the labor force participation rate of adults aged 25 to 54 the decline disappears

- Two major trends that is not reflected an economic statistics:
	1. Advent of encrypted technologies
	- Cryptocurrency
	2. New ways of earning money by creating and selling digital assets and services using websites and app. 
	- Gig economy, offers people, more flexibility, autonomy, and leisure time 

#### SO WHERE ARE WE HEADED?
- Fundamental issues to consider
	- First employment is not an end of itself, but it means to an end
 		- One goal of work is to meet the material needs of life, but as AI unlocks, unprecedented material abundance across countless areas, the struggle for physical survival will fade into history
		- Another goal of work is to give purpose the meaning to life
			- AI will empower humans to offer contributions far beyond what has ever been possible before
				- Image creating AIs like Midjourney and DALL-E will expand over to music, video, and games radically democratizing creative expression
- AI will eradicate many jobs near the bottom of the skill ladder and this trend will pick up speed
	- Primary method to improving human skill has been education.
- With exponentially growing GDP, social safety net spending will also continue to increase overall and per capita
	- During 2030s same level of financial support will provide a very comfortable standard of living w/o needing to further increase the budget
	- Growing abundance will lower the incentives for violence.
- Universal Basic Income(UBI)
	- We would definitely have UBI, in early 2030s in developed countries
	- It would entail regular payments to all adults or provision of free goods and services funded by some combination of taxes on automation driven profits and government investments in emerging tech
- Technological abundance doesn't automatically benefit everyone equally
	- Therefore we'll need smart govt policies to ease the transition and ensure that prosperity is shared among society
	- However, if we are not careful as a society toxic politics could interfere with producing good policies
	- we will need an engaged public and sensible governance to manage it
As we enter the era of abundance, we are moving up Maslow's hierarchy of needs.
	- ppl often say that death and the brevity of our existence gives meaning to life but this is an attempt to rationalize the tragedy of death as a good thing.
	- Death takes from us all things that give life meaning: skills, experiences, memories, relationships.
	- It prevents us from enjoying more of transcendent moments that define us like producing and appreciating creative works, expressing a loving sentiment, sharing humor

- Daniel Kahneman envisions a protracted period of conflict and even violence.
	- He thinks there will be winners and losers as automation continues its impact
	- A driver losing a job will not be satisfied by the promise of humanity moving up the heirarchy of life as he may not be able to make that transition.
	- Kahneman and Kurzweil agree that a lot of the polarization we see in politics today is the product of automation
	- Kahneman argues social safety net doesnt replace the sense of purpose jobs give

- We are not in competition with AI rather it will feel like an augmentation of us.
	- Just as no one thinks we are in competition with the smartphone. Its only a tool that empowers us.
	- With nanotech the augmentation of the future will feel even more natural bc it will actually be an extension of our brain.

- Orchestrating a constructive transition will requre enlightened political strategies and policy decisions.
